Little vs A little

Uncountable
little
+ uncountable noun
Negative meaning = NOT ENOUGH 😟
There's little milk left in the fridge. We should buy some today.
He has little experience with baking, but he's willing to give it a try. (= not much experience)
There's little hope of finding survivors after the accident.
a little
+ uncountable noun
Positive meaning = ENOUGH 😊
There is a little milk left in the fridge, so we don't need to buy any more today.
There is a little milk left, but it should be enough for my morning coffee. (= some milk)
She has a little time before the meeting — enough to grab coffee.

Key rule

  • Both little and a little go with uncountable nouns (milk, money, time, experience…)
  • little = not much → feels negative, like a problem
  • a little = some → feels positive, sufficient amount
  • The article a completely changes the feeling of the sentence!

Few vs A few

Countable plural
few
+ countable plural
Negative meaning = NOT ENOUGH 😟
There were few customers, so we decided to close the shop early.
There are few vegetables in the fridge to make a nutritious salad, it would be a good idea to buy some more. (= not many vegetables)
Few students passed the exam. The teacher was disappointed.
a few
+ countable plural
Positive meaning = ENOUGH 😊
There were a few customers on the first day, so we made some money.
I have a few cookbooks that I often refer to for new recipes. (= some cookbooks)
I need a few minutes to finish this — I'll be right with you.

Key rule

  • Both few and a few go with countable plural nouns (customers, books, friends…)
  • few = not many → feels negative, not enough
  • a few = some → feels positive, a small but sufficient number
  • Same trick as little/a little: the article a makes all the difference!
few / little a few / a little
Noun type few → plural countable
little → uncountable		 a few → plural countable
a little → uncountable
Feeling Negative — not enough Positive — some, sufficient
Memory trick "There's little hope…" — gloomy! "There's a little hope…" — optimistic!

Much & Many

Questions & negatives
much
a large amount of something
uncountable
Mainly: negatives & questions
Did you consume much sugar in your dessert?
I didn't eat much for dinner.
There isn't much time left — hurry!
Alone (no noun)
They go out for dinner a lot. (NOT "much" alone ✗)
I don't have much — only a little.
many
a large number of people/things
countable plural
Mainly: negatives & questions
There weren't many options for vegetarian dishes on the menu.
How many people attended the food festival?
Also OK in positive sentences
Many people attended the food festival last weekend.
She has many friends from her university years.

Much vs Many — quick rule

  • much = with uncountable nouns (water, sugar, time, money)
  • many = with countable plural nouns (people, books, ideas)
  • Both prefer negative sentences and questions
  • In positive sentences → use a lot of instead (sounds more natural)
✔I don't have much money this week.
✔There aren't many seats left.
✔I have a lot of money this week. (positive → a lot of)
✘I have much money this week. ← sounds unnatural in everyday speech

A lot of / Lots of / Plenty of / Loads of

Both noun types
a lot of
a large quantity / number
both types
Positive, negative, questions
A lot of people enjoy indulging in a sweet treat after dinner.
She has a lot of energy because she eats a balanced breakfast.
I don't have a lot of time, but I'll try to help.
lots of
informal = a lot of
both types
Informal / spoken
Lots of people enjoy indulging in a sweet treat after dinner.
She has lots of friends from school.
They have lots of money saved up for the trip.
plenty of
more than enough
both types
= more than enough → positive
Plenty of nutrients can be found in leafy greens like spinach.
Don't worry — there's plenty of time before the deadline.
We have plenty of chairs for everyone.
loads of
same as a lot of — very informal
both types
Very informal / spoken only
She has loads of energy because she eats a balanced breakfast every morning.
They have loads of fun at their parties.
He spent loads of money on the renovation.

Without a noun — use "a lot" (drop "of")

  • When there is no noun after, drop "of" → use just a lot
  • They go out for dinner a lot. ✔ (NOT "a lot of" ✗)
  • Formality scale: a lot of (neutral) → lots of (informal) → loads of (very informal)
  • plenty of = specifically "more than enough" — emphasises sufficiency

Too much & Too many

Always negative!
too much
more than we need / want — a problem
uncountable
Always = problem / excessive
He had too much caffeine during the day, making it difficult for him to fall asleep at night.
I ate too much at dinner — I feel sick.
There's too much noise in here to concentrate.
too many
more than we need / want — a problem
countable plural
Always = problem / excessive
He consumed too many high-calorie desserts, causing him to put on weight.
There are too many people in this room — it's overcrowded.
She made too many mistakes in the test.

⚠ Remember — "too" is ALWAYS negative

too much (uncountable):
too much sugar, too much time, too much money, too much noise
too many (countable plural):
too many people, too many cars, too many questions, too many calories

Enough & Not enough

Both noun types
enough
as much / as many as you need
both types
Positive — just right
There is enough protein in this meal to meet your daily requirements.
Do we have enough chairs for everyone?
She's old enough to make her own decisions.
not enough
less than you need
both types
Negative — insufficient
There are not enough servings of fruits and vegetables in your diet.
I don't have enough time to finish the report today.
There aren't enough seats on the train.
Where to place "enough" in a sentence
1

Before a NOUN

meaning "all that is necessary"

I don't have enough ingredients to make a cake.
We need enough money to pay the bills.
2

After a VERB (no object)

stands alone at the end

I've eaten enough; I don't want any more dessert.
Have you slept enough?
3

After an ADJECTIVE / ADVERB

always comes after, never before

This soup is hot enough for me.
The steak was cooked well enough.
She's tall enough to reach the shelf.
✔Is it hot enough? (adjective + enough)
✔I have enough time to finish. (enough + noun)
✘Is it enough hot? ← wrong order with adjectives
✘I have enough of time. ← no "of" before a noun

Conditionals

Умовні речення — нульовий, перший, другий, третій типи та змішані

Zero First Second Third Mixed Unless Compare all Mistakes
Reality & probability scale — where each conditional lives
Zero Always true
First Likely / real
Second Unlikely / unreal
Third Impossible (past)
Mixed Past → present
0
Zero Conditional
General truths & facts
Used for things that are always true — scientific facts, natural laws, habits, instructions.
Formula
If / When + Present Simple , Present Simple
✦ "When" can always replace "if" — both clauses describe something that always happens.
✦ The IF clause can come first or second (comma used only when IF comes first).
🔬
Scientific facts
Laws of nature that never change. The result is 100% guaranteed.
🌡️
Natural processes
Cause-and-effect relationships in the physical world.
🔄
Habits & routines
Things that happen every time, automatically, by habit.
📋
Instructions & rules
What to do in a situation — recipes, manuals, instructions.
Examples
science
If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
= always true, physical law
science
If you mix red and blue, you get purple.
= guaranteed result every time
nature
Plants die if they don't get water.
= universal truth
nature
When it rains, the ground gets wet.
= "when" = "if" in zero conditional
habit
If she drinks coffee late, she doesn't sleep.
= her personal pattern, always
instruction
If the screen freezes, press the reset button.
= general instruction / manual
If you touch fire, you get burned.
IF + present simple present simple general truth
Both clauses in present simple. The result always follows the condition — no exceptions.
If you don't eat well, your body doesn't function properly.
IF + don't/doesn't don't/doesn't still always true
Negative zero conditional: negate both verbs with don't / doesn't. The truth still holds.
What happens if you mix vinegar and baking soda?
What + present simple asking about a law/process
Questions about general truths or processes. Useful in science, cooking, technology contexts.
1
First Conditional
Real & likely situations
Used for real possibilities in the future — things that could genuinely happen. The speaker believes it's realistic.
Formula
If + Present Simple , will + base verb
✦ Never use "will" in the IF clause — use present simple even though you mean the future.
✦ Can also use: can / may / might / should / must instead of "will" in the main clause.
✦ Use "going to" instead of "will" for planned actions: If it rains, I'm going to cancel the trip.
⚠️
Warnings
Alerting someone to a real future danger or consequence.
🤝
Promises
Committing to do something if a condition is met.
🗺️
Plans & decisions
What you'll do depending on how a situation turns out.
💡
Suggestions & offers
Offering help or advice based on a likely situation.
🔮
Predictions
Predicting what will happen if something occurs.
📢
Threats
Stating negative consequences of an action.
Examples
warning
If you touch that wire, you will get hurt.
= specific, real current danger
promise
If you help me move, I will buy you dinner.
= a real commitment
plan
If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.
= a real future possibility
prediction
If she studies hard, she will pass the exam.
= likely outcome
modal: might
If the weather is nice, we might go to the beach.
= less certain than "will"
modal: should
If you feel ill, you should see a doctor.
= advice / recommendation
modal: can
If you finish early, you can leave.
= permission
going to
If the traffic is bad, I 'm going to take the metro.
= planned intention
If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.
IF + present simple will + base verb real possibility
The condition is realistic — rain tomorrow is possible. The speaker is planning based on it.
If she doesn't call by 6, I won't wait any longer.
IF + don't/doesn't won't + base verb negative consequence
Negative first conditional: negate the IF clause with don't/doesn't, and the main clause with won't.
What will you do if you don't get the job?
What will + subject + do asking about future plans
First conditional questions ask about real future possibilities — the asker genuinely wants to know the plan.
If you feel tired, you might want to rest.
might / can / should / must softens the certainty
will = certain · might/may = possible · can = permission/ability · should = advice · must = obligation
2
Second Conditional
Unreal & imaginary present/future
Used for imaginary situations — things that are unlikely, impossible, or hypothetical. The speaker does NOT expect this to happen.
Formula
If + Past Simple , would + base verb
✦ Use "were" (not "was") for all persons in formal/written English: If I were you… · If she were here…
✦ "Was" is acceptable in informal speech: If I was rich…
✦ Can also use: could / might instead of "would" in the main clause.
💭
Daydreams & wishes
Imagining a different, better reality — things you wish were true.
🎭
Hypothetical situations
Discussing what WOULD happen in an imaginary scenario.
🤔
Advice: "If I were you…"
The most common way to give polite advice in English.
🏔️
Unlikely / impossible now
Things that are very unlikely or impossible in the present situation.
Examples
wish / dream
If I had a million dollars, I would travel the world.
= I don't have a million dollars (unreal)
wish / dream
If I were a bird, I would fly to the ocean.
= impossible, but fun to imagine
advice
If I were you, I would apologise immediately.
= classic advice formula — very polite
hypothetical
What would you do if you won the lottery?
= imaginary scenario question
unlikely
If he spoke better English, he could get that job.
= currently he doesn't speak well enough
modal: might
If we left now, we might catch the last train.
= possible but not certain
⚡ First vs Second — key difference
FIRST — real, likely
If it rains tomorrow, I will cancel the barbecue.
→ Rain is possible. The speaker genuinely expects it might rain.
SECOND — unreal, unlikely
If it rained every day, I would move to Spain.
→ It doesn't rain every day. This is imaginary / hypothetical.
3
Third Conditional
Unreal past — regrets & criticism
Used for imagining a different past — things that did NOT happen. Expresses regrets, criticism, or speculation about past events.
Formula
If + Past Perfect , would have + past participle
✦ Past Perfect = had + past participle (had gone, had known, had studied)
✦ Can also use: could have / might have instead of "would have"
✦ Contractions: would've, could've, might've — very common in speech
✦ "If I had known…" → shortcut: "Had I known…" (formal inversion, no "if")
😔
Regrets
Wishing you had done something differently in the past.
😤
Criticism
Pointing out what someone should or shouldn't have done.
🔎
Speculation
Imagining how a past situation could have been different.
📜
Historical "what ifs"
Discussing how history might have unfolded differently.
Examples
regret
If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.
= I didn't study hard → I failed (real past)
regret
If she had left earlier, she wouldn't have missed the train.
= she left late → she missed it
criticism
If you had listened to me, this wouldn't have happened.
= you didn't listen → problem occurred
speculation
If he had invested in Apple in 2000, he would have become very rich.
= he didn't invest (historical what-if)
could have
If we had booked earlier, we could have got cheaper tickets.
= possibility in the past
inversion (formal)
Had I known about the problem, I would have fixed it.
= Had I known = If I had known
If I had known you were coming, I would have cooked dinner.
had + past participle would have + p.p. past — impossible to change
Reality: I didn't know you were coming, so I didn't cook dinner. The past is over and cannot be changed.
If I'd known you were coming, I would've cooked dinner.
I'd = I had would've = would have natural in speech
In spoken English, contractions are very common. I'd (I had), would've, could've, might've — all normal and natural.
Had I known you were coming, I would have cooked dinner.
Had + subject + past participle formal / literary
Inversion: drop "if" and put "had" before the subject. Same meaning, more formal. Used in writing, speeches, literature.
Mix
Mixed Conditionals
Combining past & present
Used when the time of the condition and the result are different — mixing elements of 2nd and 3rd conditionals.
Type A — Past condition, present result
If + Past Perfect , would + base verb
Past event → effect still felt now
Examples — type A
If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.
= I didn't study medicine (past) → I'm not a doctor today (present)
If she hadn't moved abroad, she would still be working here.
= she moved (past) → she doesn't work here now (present)
If he had taken that job, he would be very wealthy now.
= past decision → present state
Type B — Present condition, past result
If + Past Simple , would have + p.p.
Current state → how it affected the past
Examples — type B
If I were braver, I would have spoken up at the meeting.
= I'm not brave (present) → I didn't speak up (past)
If she weren't so shy, she would have asked for help.
= she is shy (present) → she didn't ask (past)
If he spoke Spanish, he would have got the job in Madrid.
= he doesn't speak Spanish (present) → didn't get job (past)
Un-
less
Unless
The exception condition
Unless = "if not" — states the one exception that would prevent the result from happening. Used with zero and first conditionals.
Core meaning
Unless + positive present simple = If … not + present simple
✦ Unless you hurry = If you don't hurry
✦ Works with zero conditional (always true) AND first conditional (future, real)
✦ Unless is especially powerful in warnings, threats, and ultimatums
Unless = Zero conditional
General truths
Unless you heat it, ice stays frozen.
= If you don't heat it, ice stays frozen.
Plants die unless they get water.
= Plants die if they don't get water.
Unless = First conditional
Future / real situations
Unless you hurry, we will miss the train.
= If you don't hurry, we will miss the train.
She won't pass unless she studies.
= She won't pass if she doesn't study.
Special rules & traps
🚫
NEVER use "not" after "unless" — it creates a double negative ✘ Unless you don't study, you'll fail. ← wrong! ✔ Unless you study, you'll fail.
🚫
NEVER use "will" after "unless" — same rule as with "if" ✘ Unless you will call me, I'll leave. ← wrong! ✔ Unless you call me, I'll leave.
↔️
Unless can come first or second — both positions are correct ✔ Unless it rains, we'll go to the park. (comma needed) ✔ We'll go to the park unless it rains. (no comma needed)
⚡
Unless is especially strong in warnings & ultimatums ✔ Unless you apologise right now, I'm leaving. ✔ Unless you pay by Friday, we will cancel your account.
❌
Unless is NOT used for past unreal / hypothetical situations ✘ Unless she had known, she wouldn't have come. ← unnatural ✔ If she hadn't known, she wouldn't have come. ← correct
Situation Unless… If … not…
Warning Unless you wear a helmet, you could get hurt. If you don't wear a helmet, you could get hurt.
Threat / ultimatum Unless you stop, I will call the police. If you don't stop, I will call the police.
Condition Unless it stops raining, the match is cancelled. If it doesn't stop raining, the match is cancelled.
General truth Unless you exercise, your health declines. If you don't exercise, your health declines.

Common Mistakes
What everyone gets wrong
⚡ Will in the IF clause
✘If it will rain tomorrow, we will cancel it.
✔If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel it.
Never use "will" in the IF clause — use present simple for all conditional types.
✘If she will agree, we can start.
✔If she agrees, we can start.
Even though the meaning is future, the IF clause uses present simple.
🔀 Was vs Were in second conditional
informalIf I was you, I would leave.
✔If I were you, I would leave.
"Were" is correct for all subjects in formal English. "Was" is accepted in casual speech only.
informalIf he was taller, he would be a model.
✔If he were taller, he would be a model.
Subjunctive "were" is used in formal/written second conditional.
🕰️ Would in the IF clause
✘If I would have money, I'd travel.
✔If I had money, I would travel.
No "would" in the IF clause — this is a very common mistake for learners.
✘If she would have called, I'd have come.
✔If she had called, I would have come.
Past perfect in IF clause, "would have" in main clause — never "would have" in IF clause.
🚫 "Not" after unless
✘Unless you don't study, you'll fail.
✔Unless you study, you'll fail.
Unless already contains the negative meaning. Adding "not" creates a double negative.
✘Unless it won't rain, we'll go out.
✔Unless it rains, we'll go out.
Never use "will" or negative forms after "unless" — use positive present simple.
📅 Second vs Third confusion
wrong tenseIf I studied harder, I would have passed.
✔ thirdIf I had studied harder, I would have passed.
For past situations: use past perfect in IF clause. Past simple = second conditional (present/future).
🔀 Comma placement
✘I will help you if you ask me.
✔If you ask me, I will help you.
Use a comma ONLY when the IF clause comes first. No comma when the main clause comes first.

Present Tenses
What is happening
now & in general
Four present tenses cover habits, ongoing actions, recently completed events and long-running ongoing situations. Each has a distinct feeling and purpose.
Present Simple
Facts, habits, routines, general truths
habits facts schedules
+ Subject+V (/ V+s/es) – don't / doesn't + V ? Do / Does + S + V?
When to use
Habits & routines: I wake up at 7 every day.
General truths & facts: The sun rises in the east.
Permanent states: She lives in Paris.
Fixed schedules: The train leaves at 9:15.
Instructions & directions: You turn left at the lights.
Newspaper headlines: President signs new law.
Storytelling / commentary: She opens the door and…
Examples
  • I drink coffee every morning.
    habit
  • Water boils at 100 degrees.
    scientific fact
  • She doesn't eat meat.
    permanent fact / choice
  • Does he work on Saturdays?
    question about routine
  • The film starts at 8 pm.
    fixed schedule
⚠ Spelling rules for he/she/it (+s)
  • Most verbs: just add -s → work→works, play→plays, eat→eats
  • Verbs ending -ch, -sh, -ss, -x, -o: add -es → watch→watches, go→goes, fix→fixes
  • Verbs ending consonant + y: change y→i, add -es → study→studies, carry→carries
  • Irregulars: be → is · have → has · do → does
Signal words alwaysusuallyoftensometimesneverevery day / weekin the morningon Mondays
Present Continuous
Actions happening right now or around now; future arrangements
right now temporary arrangements
+ am / is / are+V+ing – am/is/are + not + V+ing ? Am/Is/Are + S + V+ing?
When to use
Right now: She is reading at this moment.
Temporary situations: I am staying at my friend's place this week.
Trends & changes: The climate is getting warmer.
Future arrangements: We are meeting at 7 tonight.
Annoying habits (+always): He is always losing his keys!
Examples
  • I am writing an email right now.
    action at this moment
  • She is living in London temporarily.
    temporary state
  • They are getting married next June.
    future arrangement
  • He is always interrupting me!
    annoying habit (irritation)
  • More people are working from home.
    trend / change
⚠ Non-continuous (stative) verbs — NEVER use with -ing
  • Emotions: love, hate, like, want, need, prefer, wish — ✘ I am loving it → ✔ I love it
  • Mental states: know, believe, understand, remember, forget, think (= opinion), mean
  • Senses: see, hear, smell, taste, seem, appear — ✘ I am seeing him → ✔ I see him
  • Possession: have (=own), own, belong, possess, contain
  • Exception! Some verbs have 2 meanings: I have a car (own) vs I am having lunch (activity)
⚠ Spelling rules for -ing
  • Short vowel + single consonant → double consonant: sit→sitting, run→running, swim→swimming
  • Silent -e → drop e: make→making, write→writing, have→having
  • Ending in -ie → change to -y: lie→lying, die→dying, tie→tying
  • Verb ending in -l (British English) → double l: travel→travelling, cancel→cancelling

Present Perfect
Past actions with a present connection; life experiences; recent events
experience result now unfinished time
+ have / has+past participle – haven't / hasn't + p.p. ? Have / Has + S + p.p.?
When to use
Life experiences (no specific time): I have been to Japan.
Result in the present: She has lost her keys (= she can't find them now).
Unfinished time period: I have worked here for 3 years.
Recent past (news): The president has resigned.
First/second/superlative: It's the best film I have ever seen.
Examples
  • I have visited Rome three times.
    life experience
  • She has broken her leg.
    result visible now
  • We haven't eaten yet.
    unfinished situation
  • Have you ever tried sushi?
    life experience question
  • He has just arrived.
    very recent past
For vs Since
  • for = duration of time
    for 3 years · for a long time · for ages
  • since = point in time
    since 2020 · since Monday · since I was born
  • I have lived here for 5 years.
    = 5 years duration
  • I have lived here since 2019.
    = started 2019, still now
⚠ Present Perfect vs Past Simple — key difference
  • Present Perfect — no specific time / time period includes now: I have seen that film. / She has lived here for years.
  • Past Simple — specific time in the past (finished): I saw that film last week. / She lived there in 2010.
  • Never use PP with: yesterday, last week, in 2005, an hour ago, when I was young
  • Use PP with: ever, never, already, yet, just, recently, so far, since, for

Present Perfect Continuous
An action that started in the past, continued up to now — emphasises duration or visible result
how long visible result still ongoing
+ have / has+been+V+ing – haven't/hasn't + been + V+ing
When to use
How long: I have been waiting for an hour.
Visible present result: She looks tired — she has been working all night.
Recent activity (still happening): I have been studying Japanese lately.
Explaining a current situation: Why is your shirt wet? I have been swimming.
Examples + PP vs PPC
  • I have been reading this book for 2 weeks.
    still reading — not finished
  • She has been crying — her eyes are red.
    visible evidence of activity
  • I have read 3 chapters. (PP)
    completed — focuses on result/number
  • I have been reading all day. (PPC)
    ongoing — focuses on duration
⚠ PP vs Present Perfect Continuous
  • Use PP for completed actions / quantity / results: I've written 3 emails.
  • Use PPC for duration / ongoing / visible evidence: I've been writing emails all morning.
  • Stative verbs (know, love, want…) use PP even for duration: I have known her for years (NOT have been knowing)

Past Tenses
What happened
before now
Four past tenses distinguish completed actions, ongoing past situations, actions before other past actions, and durations in the past.
Past Simple
Completed actions at a specific time in the past
completed specific time sequence
+ Subject + V2 (past form) – didn't + V (base) ? Did + S + V (base)?
When to use
Completed past action: She left at 8.
Series of completed events: I woke up, showered and had breakfast.
Specific time in past: We met in 2018.
Past habits (with "used to"): I used to play football every weekend.
Storytelling / narrative: She opened the door and saw him.
Examples
  • I visited Paris last summer.
    specific time
  • She didn't come to the party.
    completed — didn't happen
  • Did you see the match yesterday?
    question about specific past
  • He woke up, dressed and left.
    sequence of events
  • I used to smoke but I stopped.
    past habit, no longer true
⚠ Irregular verbs — no rule, must memorise

  • see → saw → seen
  • have → had → had
  • make → made → made
  • take → took → taken
  • come → came → come
  • give → gave → given
  • know → knew → known
  • think → thought → thought
  • get → got → got/gotten
  • say → said → said
  • write → wrote → written
  • buy → bought → bought
  • bring → brought → brought
  • find → found → found
  • feel → felt → felt
  • begin → began → begun
  • break → broke → broken
  • fly → flew → flown
  • speak → spoke → spoken

Past Continuous
An ongoing action at a specific moment in the past; interrupted actions; background in stories
at that moment interrupted background
+ was / were+V+ing – wasn't/weren't + V+ing ? Was/Were + S + V+ing?
When to use
At a specific past moment: At 8 pm I was cooking dinner.
Interrupted by shorter action (when): I was sleeping when the phone rang.
Two parallel past actions (while): She was reading while he was cooking.
Background in a story: It was raining. The streets were shining…
Annoying past habits (+always): He was always losing his phone.
Examples
  • I was watching TV when she called.
    interrupted action — PC + PS
  • They were playing football all afternoon.
    ongoing at a past time
  • While I was cooking, he was cleaning.
    two simultaneous past actions
  • The sun was shining and birds were singing.
    story background / setting
  • What were you doing at 9 pm?
    question about past moment
⚠ When vs While
  • When + short action (Past Simple): I was reading when the door opened.
  • While + ongoing action (Past Continuous): While I was reading, she was sleeping.
  • Both clauses Past Continuous = two parallel activities: While he was singing, she was dancing.
Signal words whenwhileasat that momentat 8 pm yesterdayall day long
Past Perfect
An action completed BEFORE another past action — the "past of the past"
before past sequence reported speech
+ had+past participle – hadn't + past participle ? Had + S + past participle?
When to use
Before another past action: When I arrived, she had already left.
Explaining a past situation: He was tired because he hadn't slept.
Reported speech (backshift): She said she had been there.
Third conditional: If I had known, I would have helped.
Wishes about the past: I wish I had studied harder.
Examples
  • By the time we arrived, the film had started.
    film started first, then we arrived
  • She had never seen snow before that winter.
    experience before a past point
  • I couldn't pay — I had forgotten my wallet.
    reason in the past
  • He said he had finished the report.
    reported speech backshift
  • Had you met her before the party?
    before a past event
⚠ When Past Perfect is NOT needed
  • If the sequence of events is clear (using before/after), Past Simple is enough: I had dinner before I went out. = I had dinner before I went out.
  • When "before" or "after" already makes the order clear, both verbs can be Past Simple.
  • Past Perfect is essential when the order is not obvious or you want to emphasize the earlier event.

Past Perfect Continuous
An action that was ongoing up to a point in the past — emphasises duration
duration before past cause in past
+ had+been+V+ing – hadn't been + V+ing
When to use
Duration before a past point: She had been waiting for 2 hours when he arrived.
Cause of a past situation: He was exhausted — he had been working all night.
Recently stopped but with visible effect: Her eyes were red. She had been crying.
Examples

    duration up to past moment
  • They had been arguing, so the atmosphere was tense.
    explains past state
  • How long had you been waiting?
    question about past duration
  • The ground was wet — it had been raining.
    evidence of earlier action

Future Tenses
What will happen
after now
English has multiple ways to express the future — will, going to, present continuous and future perfect each carry different nuances of intention, prediction and completion.
Future Simple (will)
Spontaneous decisions, predictions, promises, offers, facts about the future
will spontaneous prediction promise
+ will + base verb – won't + base verb ? Will + S + base verb? also be going to + base verb
Will — use when
Spontaneous decision: I'm cold. I will close the window.
Promise: I will call you tomorrow.
Prediction (opinion): I think it will rain.
Offer: I will help you with that.
Future fact: She will be 30 next year.
Request: Will you help me?
Going to — use when
Prior intention/plan: I am going to study medicine. (decided)
Prediction with evidence: Look at those clouds — it is going to rain.
Inevitable outcome: She is going to have a baby (visible).
Decided before now: We are going to move house next month.
Examples — will vs going to
  • A: The phone is ringing! B: I will get it.
    spontaneous — will
  • I am going to buy a new car. I've already saved the money.
    prior plan — going to
  • I think Barcelona will win.
    opinion prediction — will
  • Watch out! You are going to fall!
    evidence-based — going to
  • I won't tell anyone. I promise.
    promise — will
⚠ Will vs Going To — key difference
  • Will = decision made AT the moment of speaking (spontaneous) OR general future prediction without specific evidence
  • Going to = decision made BEFORE speaking (prior intention) OR prediction based on visible evidence/signs
  • Future plans with exact time → prefer Present Continuous: The train is leaving at 8. / We are having dinner at 7.
Signal words tomorrownext week/yearsoonin the futureI think/believeprobablymaybe
Future Continuous
An action that will be in progress at a specific future moment
in progress at future moment polite questions
+ will be+V+ing – won't be + V+ing ? Will + S + be + V+ing?
When to use
In progress at future moment: At 9 pm I will be sleeping.
Planned / expected action: I will be meeting the client tomorrow.
Polite / indirect question: Will you be joining us for dinner?
Parallel future actions: While I will be cooking, she will be shopping.
Examples
  • This time tomorrow I will be flying to Tokyo.
    in progress at a future moment
  • She will be working when you arrive.
    ongoing at future time
  • Will you be using the car tonight?
    polite question (no pressure)
  • Don't call at 8 — I will be having dinner.
    expected activity at that time
Signal words at this time tomorrowthis time next weekwhen you arrivestillat … o'clock
Future Perfect
An action that will be completed BEFORE a specific future point
completed before by a deadline achievement
+ will have+past participle – won't have + p.p. ? Will + S + have + p.p.?
When to use
Completed before future deadline: I will have finished by 5 pm.
Achievement at a future point: By 2030, she will have worked here for 20 years.
Assumption about the past: She will have arrived by now.
Examples
  • I will have graduated by next June.
    completed before future point
  • By the time you read this, I will have left.
    before another future event
  • She will have saved enough money by December.
    achievement by deadline
  • Will you have finished by 3 pm?
    question about completion

Future Perfect Continuous
An action that will have been in progress for a duration up to a specific future point
how long by then duration + future
+ will have been+V+ing – won't have been + V+ing
When to use
Duration up to future point: By May, I will have been studying English for 5 years.
Explaining a future state: She will be tired — she will have been travelling all day.
Emphasis on the activity's ongoing nature: How long will you have been working here by retirement?
Examples
  • By next year, we will have been living here for a decade.
    duration until future moment
  • When she retires, she will have been teaching for 35 years.
    achievement of duration
  • I will have been waiting for 3 hours by the time he arrives.
    ongoing up to future moment
Note: This is the rarest tense in everyday English
  • Used mainly in formal/written contexts or for emphasis on duration.
  • In conversation, Future Perfect is usually preferred: By May I'll have studied English for 5 years.

Common Mistakes
What everyone
gets wrong
The most frequent tense errors learners make — with corrections and explanations.
Present tense mistakes
✘She is knowing the answer.
✔She knows the answer.
Stative verb "know" — never use with -ing.
✘I have seen him yesterday.
✔I saw him yesterday.
Specific past time → Past Simple, not Present Perfect.
✘She work in a bank.
✔She works in a bank.
He/She/It → add -s to the verb in Present Simple.
Past tense mistakes
✘I didn't went to the party.
✔I didn't go to the party.
After did/didn't — use the base verb, not past form.
✘When I arrived, she left.
✔When I arrived, she had already left.
She left before I arrived — use Past Perfect for the earlier action.
✘I was study all night.
✔I was studying all night.
Past Continuous = was/were + V-ING (not base verb).
Future tense mistakes
✘I will to go to the shops.
✔I will go to the shops.
After will — use the base verb (no "to").
✘I'm going to will travel next year.
✔I'm going to travel / I will travel next year.
Don't mix will and going to together.
✘If it will rain, I'll stay home.
✔If it rains, I'll stay home.
In conditional "if" clauses — use Present Simple, not will.
Perfect tense mistakes
✘I have been to Paris last year.
✔I went to Paris last year.
"Last year" is a specific time → Past Simple.
✘She has gone to the shops. (she's here now)
✔She has been to the shops.
Has gone = she is still away. Has been = she went and came back.
✘I am working here since 2018.
✔I have been working here since 2018.
Duration from past to present → Present Perfect (Continuous).

Passive Voice

Master the passive voice in American English — when to use it, how to form it, and common pitfalls to avoid.

What Tenses When Rules American Quiz
Section 1

What is Passive Voice?

In active voice, the subject does the action. In passive voice, the subject receives the action.

Active: The chef cooks the meal.
Passive: The meal is cooked by the chef.

We form the passive with the correct tense of "be" + past participle (V3). The agent can be added with "by", or omitted.

🔶
Active VoiceSubject + Verb + Object
Tom writes emails.
🟢
Passive VoiceObject → Subject + be + V3 (+ by agent)
Emails are written by Tom.

Section 3

When to Use Passive Voice

1

The doer is unknown or unimportant

When we don't know who did the action, or it's not relevant.

"My car was stolen last night."We don't know who stole it.
"English is spoken all over the world."Who speaks it is obvious/unimportant.
2

The action/result is more important

In science, news, and formal writing, we focus on what happened.

"The experiment was conducted in 2023."Focus: the experiment, not the scientists.
"Three people were injured."News style: focus on the victims.
💡
Don't overuse passive!In American English, active voice is preferred for clear, direct writing. Use passive only when there's a good reason.
Section 4

Important Rules

🔄 Multiple Passives from One Active Sentence

When an active sentence has two objects (direct + indirect), you can make two different passive sentences.

Active: The teacher gave the students a test.

Passive 1: The students were given a test.
Passive 2: A test was given to the students.
⭐
In American English, Passive 1 (person as subject) is much more common.

⛔ Intransitive Verbs Cannot Be Passive

Verbs without an object have no passive form.

🚫
No passive for:
  • appear — It appeared suddenly. (NOT: "It was appeared.")
  • die — He died yesterday. (NOT: "He was died.")
  • fall — The tree fell. (NOT: "The tree was fallen.")
  • go — She went home. (NOT: "Home was gone to.")
  • happen — An accident happened. (NOT: "An accident was happened.")
  • arrive, sleep, come, exist, live, sit

❓ Questions in Passive Voice

Invert the subject and auxiliary "be". The agent goes to the end.

Was the email sent by the manager?
Is the project being reviewed?
Has the report been submitted?

Wh-questions:

Active: Who wrote this book?
Passive: Who was this book written by?

Active: When did they build this bridge?
Passive: When was this bridge built?

🧩 Passive with Infinitive and Gerund

Modal Verbs + Bare Infinitive

⚙
modal + be + V3
The report can be finished today.
This issue must be resolved immediately.

Need / Want / Going to + To-Infinitive

📋
need / want / going + to be + V3
The car needs to be repaired.
The building is going to be demolished.

Enjoy / Hate / Like + Being + V3

🎭
verb + being + V3
Nobody enjoys being criticized.
She hates being ignored.

⚡ GET-Passive (Informal)

In spoken American English, "get" often replaces "be".

He got fired last week. (= was fired)
She got promoted to manager.
My phone got stolen at the concert.
⚠
GET-passive rules:
  • Only action verbs, not state verbs.
  • ✅ get injured, get caught, get invited
  • ❌ NOT: get known, get believed, get owned
  • Often implies something unexpected or negative.
  • Common: get married, get divorced, get lost, get dressed

🔗 Phrasal Verbs in Passive

The preposition/particle stays attached to the verb.

Active: They called off the meeting.
Passive: The meeting was called off. ✅
Active: Someone broke into the store.
Passive: The store was broken into.
Active: They looked after the children.
Passive: The children were looked after.
Section 5

American English Notes

🇺🇸 American vs. British Differences

  • "Gotten" vs. "Got": In AmE, past participle is often gotten in perfect tenses: "He has gotten promoted." BrE uses got. In GET-passive, both use got: "He got promoted."
  • Active voice preference: American style guides (AP, Chicago) strongly favor active voice. Use passive only when the agent is truly unknown or when the action matters more.
  • GET-passive is more common: He got arrested sounds perfectly natural in AmE, while BrE might prefer He was arrested.
  • "By" often dropped: In conversational AmE, the "by" phrase is usually omitted unless the agent is important or surprising.
  • Past participle forms: proven (AmE) vs. proved (BrE), gotten vs. got, stricken (AmE formal) vs. struck (BrE).
  • Informal contractions: "It's been done" instead of "It has been done", "He's being watched" instead of "He is being watched."
